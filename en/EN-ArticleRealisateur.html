A visionary of cinematic action

David Leitch is an American director, producer and former stuntman, renowned for his unique style and ability to combine action, humor and visual aesthetics in his films. He first made his mark as co-director of “John Wick” (2014), which redefined the action film genre with its fluid and stylized fight choreography. Since then, Leitch has continued to gain notoriety with projects such as “Atomic Blonde” (2017) and “Deadpool 2” (2018), demonstrating his mastery of action sequences and talent for dynamic storytelling.

Between action and confinement: The railway world of David Leitch

His latest film, Bullet Train (2022), showcases his singular vision of trains as action set-pieces. In “Bullet Train”, a group of assassins find themselves on board a high-speed train, where tensions rise as their paths cross. Leitch uses the train not only as a means of transport, but also as a confined space that heightens the suspense and intensity of the confrontations. The dynamics of the moving train make for an inventive stage direction, with action scenes that exploit the unique characteristics of this environment: narrow corridors, compartments and landscapes that speed by.

The train as a metaphor for intertwined destinies

Leitch also illustrates how trains can serve as metaphors for the characters' life journeys. In “Bullet Train”, each killer has his own story and his own motivations, and the train becomes the place where these stories meet and intertwine. The speed of the train, which evokes a certain inevitability, reflects the choices and consequences that each character must face.

The train as narrator: a journey between history and the future

By integrating these visual and narrative elements, David Leitch presents the train not just as a simple means of transport, but as a symbol of movement and destiny. This choice of setting also underlines the link between the past and the future of rail transport, echoing the evolution of trains from the first steam locomotives to futuristic plans for magnetic levitation trains. With “Bullet Train”, Leitch invites us to consider how the train, while a symbol of travel, can also represent complex human experiences and intertwined narratives.
